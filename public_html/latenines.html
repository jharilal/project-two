Downfall of Netscape

The internet began to exponentially increased in size by the time 1996 had come around. Average consumers had began to use it and this led to the browser war between Microsoft and Netscape to have higher stakes. As a final nail in the coffin for Netscape, Microsoft included Internet Explorer free on all windows computers. Netscape could no longer compete with the giant, and led to a crash in Netscape Stock, which would eventually fade out of existence. Microsoft's browser had reigned victorious.

Blowing (e)Bubbles

In 1997,Amazon IPO'd on the NYSE. Bezos saw Amazon as an opportunity to establish trust between the consumer and the company by the user trusting the company with credit card information. New advancements in fiber optics and the silicon chip had allowed for quicker movement of data and money, which allowed for more money to be tossed around at eCommerce companies. By 1999, there were 250 eCommerce companies public on the NYSE.

A New Kind of Search

Sergey Brin and Larry Page launch Google in 1998. Google at the time was a search engine that was unlike the competitors, since it was based off of "popularity contest" between website links. Google had wanted to focus solely on search, so it never had any banner ads on it, and instead, generated income similar to the phonebook by the "pay for placement" ideology. Excite misses an opportunity to buy Google for sub 1-million dollars.

The End of Copyright?

In 1999, Shawn Fanning launched Napster which had a very short run, but led to important developments in the internet. Napster involved file sharing of mp3 files from peer to peer, and involved college students sharing their music taste to peers. However, this was free distribution of copyright information, which led to the prompt shutdown of Napster in 2000. Napster's lasting impact was the ability for the everyday consumer to showcase their interstes on the internet and eventually inspired social medias such as Youtube and Myspace.
